Computer Science

Thinking like a computer scientist

Computer scientists take abstract problems and goals that can be solved with a computer, and convert them into a sequence of steps (an algorithm). Once a computer scientist has formed a conceptual understanding of a problem, they can write the implementation that is carried out by a computer.

Thinking requires experience

When an average person is asked to plan a wedding, they will initially be overwhelmed - planning a big event is a complex task. A wedding planner, on the other hand, has a systematic process for completing the large task of planning a wedding as a sequence of smaller tasks. In each smaller tasks, they have a clear understanding of what is feasible, where the best resources are, and which choices yielded the best results for them in the past.

An experienced event planner can take an informal description of an event and create an implementation that can be carried out by their team. Similarly, an experienced computer scientist can take an abstract problem and devise the step-by-step procedure that a computer can follow to produce the intended result.

Thinking is writing

In any profession, thinking is expressed by some form of writing: architects sketch a blueprint, scientists put together a hypothesis, and entrepreneurs write a business plan. The way that thoughts are written formalizes the thinking patterns of the writer. In computer science, the writing used to convey ideas is called pseudocode.

Here is an example of pseudocode for a popular American song, "99 Bottles of Beer".

set n to 99
while n is greater than 0,
    sing n
    sing "bottles of beer on the wall"
    sing n
    sing "bottles of beer. Take one down, pass it around"
    sing n - 1
    sing "bottles of beer on the wall"
    reduce n by 1

Pseudocode doesn't have any formal specification - I could just as easily have written yodel instead of sing, or n = 99 instead of set n to 99. I could have even written sing 'n bottles of beer...' and have it be assumed that one would not literally sing the letter n. The sole purpose of pseudocode is to informally convey the underlying steps to perform an algorithm.

Thinking with functions

Here is another pseudocode example for singing a popular 70's song.

define singPhrase with line, as
    sing line
    sing "uh huh uh huh"

singPhrase with "that's the way"
singPhrase with "I like it"

In the pseudocode, I defined a function - a sequence of reusable instructions that can take inputs and produce outputs. The function's name is singPhrase, and it takes an input called line. The instruction singPhrase with "that's the way" is instructing the singPhrase function to use "that's the way" as the value for the word line, and perform the two instructions that are indented under singPhrase.

Thinking with repetitions

Here is another example of pseudocode to describe my daily routine.

while I am alive,
    wake up
    brush teeth
    ...
    eat dinner
    sleep

The while statement repeats the instructions indented under while I am alive as long as I am alive is a true statement. I could have effectively written this same pseudocode by simply copying and pasting the instructions as shown below.

wake up
brush teeth
...
eat dinner
sleep
wake up
brush teeth
...

The problem with the pseudocode above is that I don't know how many times I will need to copy and paste the instructions. The while I am alive instruction has a condition (I am alive) which controls whether the instructions indented under it are performed. As long as the condition is true, another repetition of the instructions is performed.

Thinking with branches

Suppose I watch a new movie every Saturday night.

while I am alive,
    wake up
    brush teeth
    ...
    eat dinner
    if day is Saturday, then
        watch a new movie
    sleep

Every day, after I eat dinner, I check what day it is. If it is Saturday, I watch a new movie, then sleep. If it's not Saturday, I don't do the watch a new movie instruction, I just jump straight to sleep. I can convey all the nuances of my life this way.

if day is Saturday or Sunday,
    wake up at 8 am
otherwise
    wake up at 6 am

Once again, we encounter a condition, which in this case is day is Saturday or Sunday. The instructions indented under the if statement are performed once if the condition is true. In the second pseudocode example, there is also another instruction following the if, otherwise, which performs a different set of instructions if the condition is false.

Here is some pseudocode to convert a percentage score to its corresponding letter grade.

if my grade is greater than 90,
    say "I got an A"
if my grade is greater than 80,
    say "I got a B"
if my grade is greater than 70,
    say "I got a C"
if my grade is greater than 60,
    say "I got a D"
if my grade is greater than 0,
    say "I got an F"

Suppose you got an 85. The first condition, my grade is greater than 90, is false, so the say "I got an A" instruction will not be performed. The second condition, my grade is greater than 80, is true, so the say "I got a B" instruction will be performed. However, the program continues and checks my grade is greater than 70, which is also true, so it will also perform say "I got a C". Likewise, the other conditions are true, so this program will incorrectly respond that your 85 got you a B, C, D, and F!

A correct version of the program above would use the otherwise keyword to imply that a condition should only be checked if no previous condition was true. Once a true condition is found, its indented instructions are performed, and no further instructions are performed.

if my grade is greater than 90,
    say "I got an A"
otherwise if my grade is greater than 80,
    say "I got a B"
otherwise if my grade is greater than 70,
    say "I got a C"
otherwise if my grade is greater than 60,
    say "I got a D"
otherwise,
    say "I got an F"

Vocabulary

Algorithm - A process or step-by-step procedure to perform a calculation or solve a problem, usually performed by a computer.

Implementation - The specific computer instructions that are used to carry out an algorithm.

Procedural programming - Breaking down the intended functionality of a program into a sequence of steps.

Function - A sequence of reusable instructions that can take inputs and produce outputs.

Pseudocode - An informal way to communicate the steps of an algorithm or process.

Code - An informal term used to refer to computer programs and instructions.

Condition - A true or false statement.

Perfect cube - An integer (whole number) that is the cube (third power) of another integer. For example, 27 is a perfect cube, since it is equal to 3 x 3 x 3.
